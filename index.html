About Uintah

The city derives its name from the Weber Ute Band of Shoshoni Indians who inhabited the area when white settlers arrived. The first Anglo-Europeans, eight Mormon families, established a settlement known as East Weber in 1850 along the Weber River. This small community grew, constructing canals for irrigation and even a fort for protection. The arrival of the railroad in 1869 marked a significant turning point, transforming the town, then briefly known as Easton, into a bustling boomtown with over 100 businesses and a population exceeding 5,000. The railroad station was named Uintah, leading to the eventual renaming of the entire town to avoid confusion. Although the relocation of the Utah Central Railroad to Ogden caused a subsequent decline, Uintah persevered and was officially incorporated as a town in 1937, continuing to celebrate its unique heritage with annual "U-Days" and the iconic hillside "U" overlooking the city.
